01 — Market context

A conversation problem disguised as a distribution problem.

36% national pad penetration sounds like a reach problem. It wasn't. Urban women knew the products existed. The barrier was stigma — periods weren't talked about, which meant they weren't shopped for openly. The brand that normalised the conversation would own the D2C cohort. That was the strategic bet Nua made, and the GTM was built entirely around proving it.

04 — Crisis management

Database leak. Customer scam. Revenue grew anyway.

Mid-tenure, Nua faced two simultaneous trust events: a customer database leak and an active scam targeting Nua buyers. In a category already built on sensitivity — women's bodies, personal health data — this was the worst possible timing. Brand trust was the entire product.

The decision

The instinct in a crisis is to go quiet — pull back content, let things settle. We did the opposite. The community had been built on radical honesty. Going silent would have felt like abandonment. The response was to lean harder into the community voice: authentic posts, direct comment engagement, real answers to real fear — not corporate statements.

What was deployed
Authentic community content — real voices, not brand voice
Direct comment engagement at scale — every concern responded to
Moment marketing to shift attention forward, not dwell on the incident
UTM-tracked influencer videos to rebuild trust through peer voices
Lucrative offers for existing community — loyalty rewarded publicly
The result

Revenue didn't drop. It grew. The community that had been built over 12 months absorbed the crisis because the trust was real — not manufactured. When you build a community on honesty, honesty in a crisis is the only credible response. This was the proof point that the entire GTM strategy was correct.

06 — Honest retrospective

What I'd do differently.

One specific miss: a period panty launch that had strong community anticipation but landed softer than expected. The content in the lead-up was education-heavy — which was right for the category — but didn't build enough product desire before the launch day. People knew why period panties mattered. They weren't convinced yet that they needed to buy one now. A pre-launch waitlist with community co-creation would have converted that awareness into intent. That learning directly informed critique 02 below.

Critique 01
SEO was an underutilised channel
Instagram-first SEO ignored
Fix

Shift 20–25% bandwidth to SEO from month 3. Search intent around period health was high and uncontested by D2C brands.

Critique 02
No pre-launch demand arc for products
Single-day launches No waitlist
Fix

3–4 week pre-launch sequence with community co-creation. Generate pull before push.

Critique 03
Attribution between content and revenue was inferred
Revenue grew 15% MoM No UTM model
Fix

UTM tracking per content format + Looker Studio dashboard linking ER to D2C conversion.

What I'd build today — retention architecture
D0 → D7 · Welcome
"Your first order is on its way. Here's what most women don't know about their cycle." Education before upsell. Sets the brand relationship.
D21 → D28 · Habit reinforcement
WhatsApp nudge: "Your next cycle in ~7 days. Your subscription ships automatically." Converts the first reorder from a decision to a default.
D60 lapsed · Community re-engagement
"A lot of women tell us X. Has that been your experience?" — re-engagement through identity, not a discount. The community voice re-activates better than the brand voice.
Target: D90 subscription retention ≥ 70%. The community already did the trust-building — retention is about removing the friction to stay subscribed, not re-selling.
The verdict

What this teardown actually proves.

What the 15× ER actually means

It means the content was doing the job that paid media usually does — building desire, reducing friction, creating the conditions for purchase. In a category where the conversation itself is the product, engagement isn't a vanity metric. It's the top of a funnel that converted at 15% MoM for a year. I learned to think about community as infrastructure, not content.

If I had month 13

I'd build the SEO layer. Search intent around period health — PCOS, cycle syncing, menstrual pain — was high and almost entirely uncontested by D2C brands. The community had already built the content. Reformatting it for search would have compounded the trust that social had earned into a channel that works while you sleep. That's the unlocked opportunity I left on the table.

Tier 2 · Should Have Activated · Full Campaign Briefs
🔍
SEO — The Channel That Works While You Sleep
MISSED
The community was building content around period health, PCOS, cycle syncing, and intimacy — all high-intent search topics that no D2C brand had owned. Every community carousel was a blog post we didn't write.
Campaign Brief
Target: "PCOS period care", "non-toxic pads India", "period cramps relief", "intimacy hygiene women" — all with low D2C competition
Format: Repurpose top-performing carousels → 1,200-word SEO articles. Zero extra production cost.
KPI: 40–60 keywords ranking page 1 by month 6. CAC from organic search vs social: target 3× lower.
My POV: Start SEO at month 3, not month 13. The community produces the content. SEO just gives it a second life with compounding returns.
📧
Email & WhatsApp — Lifecycle That Sells Subscriptions
UNDERBUILT
Nua is a subscription product. Subscription products live and die on lifecycle automation. WhatsApp in India has dramatically higher open rates than email — and Nua's audience is on their phones during the exact moments that trigger a purchase.
Full Flow Architecture
Welcome: "Your first order is on its way. Here's what most women don't know about their cycle." — Education before upsell.
Cycle reminder (WhatsApp): 5 days before predicted period. "Your kit ships tomorrow — want to add anything?" — Replenishment + upsell in one message.
Winback (D60 lapsed): Community story post that asks a question, not a discount. "A lot of women tell us X. Has that been your experience?" — re-engagement through identity, not offer.
My POV: WhatsApp lifecycle is the highest-ROI investment in Indian D2C subscription. Set up in month 1, it compounds every month after.
⚡
Paid Social — UGC-Fed Meta, Not Branded Ads
WOULD ADD
The community content and influencer UGC was performing at 10× the category average organically. None of it was being boosted or used in paid. That's leaving the best-performing creative stranded.
Campaign Brief
Top-of-funnel: Dark-post UGC (community members talking about their experience, no brand watermark). "This ad shouldn't look like an ad." — CAC target 40% below branded creative.
Retargeting: B Corp trust signal + testimonial. "India's cleanest period care — and 12,000 women who switched agree." — Removes price friction in consideration stage.
Budget split: 70% UGC/community content, 30% polished brand. Creative fatigue is lower on authentic formats.
My POV: In a category built on trust, a branded ad is always fighting an uphill battle. A real woman's voice in a dark post wins it on a flat road.
🔁
Retention + Referral — Building the Flywheel
WOULD ADD
Nua's D2C subscription model means retention is not a marketing afterthought — it's the revenue model. A customer who subscribes for 18 months has 3× the LTV of one who buys twice. Referral works when product satisfaction is genuinely high, and at Nua it was.
Retention + Referral Architecture
Loyalty: Monthly "Nua Diary" — personalised usage insights + cycle content delivered to subscribers. Makes the subscription feel alive, not just a box that shows up.
Referral: "Gift a friend her first period kit" — gifting mechanic reduces the awkwardness of recommending period products, reframes it as care. ₹150 credit per referral.
KPI: D90 retention ≥ 70%. Referral K-factor ≥ 0.3 (1 in 3 customers refers one friend within 6 months).
My POV: Referral doesn't need a discount. It needs a mechanic that makes sharing feel natural. In women's wellness, gifting is that mechanic.
