04 — Organic social

Better thinking replaced spend.

The 400% engagement lift and 80% budget cut happened simultaneously — so the improvement wasn't from spending more, it was from thinking differently. One change drove most of it: carousels were redesigned from product-first (slide 1 = product shot) to insight-first (slide 1 = a finding or question the audience didn't know they had). Swipe-through rates jumped immediately. The thing I didn't do in time: feed the top-performing organic posts into paid. That silo meant we kept rebuilding creative from scratch instead of compounding what was already working.

06 — Honest retrospective

Three things I'd do differently.

One specific miss worth naming: the smallest campaign (1.1M impressions, protein) underperformed on CTR despite strong creative. Post-analysis showed the audience targeting was too broad — OZiva's protein buyer is specific (women, 25–35, fitness-aware but not gym-native) and the targeting didn't reflect that. The three structural fixes below would have caught this earlier and faster.

Critique 01
No holdout testing for true ROAS
4.2× blended ROAS tracked Incrementality unknown
Fix

10–15% holdout audiences per campaign. Measure true incremental ROAS vs attributed ROAS.

Critique 02
Category budget not tied to LTV
Vitamins got most spend Beauty LTV ignored
Fix

Cohort-level LTV model in SQL by category. Allocate on predicted LTV, not short-term ROAS.

Critique 03
Social and paid ran in silos
400% organic ER lift Never fed into paid
Fix

Top organic posts feed a weekly "boost candidate" pool — reduces paid creative risk and compounds what's working.

The verdict

What I took out of this.

What the numbers actually mean

The 4.2× blended ROAS wasn't just good execution. It was the result of a brand that had already done half the persuasion work before the ad ran. When your positioning removes price objection, retargeting becomes confirmation, not convincing. I learned to look for that structural advantage first — and build the funnel around it, not around the creative.

If I had month 10

I'd have moved budget into beauty nutrition. The intent signals were there by month 4 — highest ATC-to-purchase ratio of the three categories, lowest spend. I didn't have the LTV model to make that case to leadership at the time. Now I'd build that model in month one, before the first budget conversation, not after.

Complete Campaign Playbook — What I'd Run

OZiva's 4.2× ROAS and 22% cart-to-purchase rate came from one structural insight: B Corp certification is a conversion tool, not a brand story. Here's the full marketing mix built around that principle — what we ran, what underperformed, and the three channels I'd add today.

⚡
Performance Marketing — Meta & Google
Ran across vitamins (55% budget), protein (30%), and beauty nutrition (15%). 4.2× blended ROAS. The key unlock: B Corp retargeting copy removed the price friction layer entirely — buyers arrived at cart already convinced.
POV + What I'd Fix
Budget allocation followed short-term ROAS, not LTV. Beauty nutrition had the highest ATC-to-purchase ratio but only 15% of budget. I'd build the cohort LTV model in month one and reallocate 20% from vitamins into beauty by month 4.

Organic Social — Insight-First Content
400% engagement lift with 80% less spend. The turning point: carousels redesigned from product-first (slide 1 = product shot) to insight-first (slide 1 = a question the audience didn't know they had). Swipe-through jumped immediately.
POV + What I'd Fix
Top-performing organic posts were never fed into paid. That silo meant we rebuilt creative from scratch instead of compounding what was working. Fix: weekly "boost candidate" pool from top organic content. Reduces paid creative risk by 60%.
🔍
SEO + Content Marketing — The Compounding Channel
OZiva was India's first B Corp nutrition brand. That certification creates organic content authority in a category where search intent — "best clean protein India", "PCOS supplements", "plant protein for women" — is high and D2C-brand competition is low.
Campaign Brief
30 category articles targeting "protein for women PCOS", "clean nutrition India", "B Corp certified supplements" — all below DR 40. Target: 50+ page-1 rankings by month 6, CAC from organic 2.5× lower than paid.
🔁
Retention & Lifecycle — Subscription Architecture
Nutrition is a replenishment category. A customer who buys HerBalance for PCOS every month for 18 months is worth 8× a one-time buyer. The entire funnel optimises toward that cohort — not the first purchase.
Lifecycle Flow
D7: education sequence on product efficacy. D21: replenishment WhatsApp nudge. D45: "How's your progress?" + upsell to bundle. D60 lapsed: B Corp re-engagement ("The same standards. Still here."). KPI: D90 retention ≥ 65%.
🤝
Influencer Seeding + Creator-Led
OZiva's B Corp certification is a "show-off" advantage — creators who care about clean nutrition are natural advocates. Seeding to fitness, wellness, and PCOS creators produces authentic content without paid rates.
Campaign Brief
50 micro-influencers (25K–100K, wellness-adjacent). Seeding only — no scripted content. Track organic mentions. Best-performing content gets paid amplification as dark posts. Target: 60% seed-to-mention rate, 3× lower CPM vs branded creative.
🛒
Marketplace Optimisation + CRO
22% cart-to-purchase vs 8% category average was driven by creative-led qualification — buyers arrived at cart pre-sold. The PDP needs to sustain that standard. Amazon A9 SEO + D2C site CRO are the conversion layer that paid media hands off to.
CRO Brief
A/B test: B Corp trust badge above the fold vs below. Hypothesis: 15%+ conversion lift for cold traffic. Mobile PDP load time target: under 2.2s. A+ content updated quarterly to match top-of-funnel creative messaging.
My POV · The Integrating Principle

OZiva's structural advantage — B Corp certification — was doing more work than we gave it credit for in the campaign architecture. The insight I'd carry forward: find the one thing about your brand that removes a friction layer from the funnel, then build every channel around amplifying it. For OZiva, that's verified trust. Build the LTV model first, allocate budget second — not the other way around.
